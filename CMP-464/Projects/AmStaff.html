The American Staffordshire Terrier

History of the American Staffordshire Terrier

The roots of the American Staffordshire Terrier go back to the 18th and 19th centuries in England. Bulldogs and terriers of the time were commonly used in blood sports (dog fighting). The blending of bulldogs and terriers made the Staffordshire Bull Terrier. Although the Staffordshire Bull Terrier was used in blood sports. It was also kept as a companion and to defend farms in those days.

Eventually, these dogs came to the United States in the mid 1800s. Breeders of these dogs in the United States created a larger Staffordshire terrier that eventually became a distinct breed of Staffordshire bull terrier, now called the American Staffordshire Terrier. American Staffordshire Terriers were bred to have a calmer and friendlier temperament than their ancestors. The American Kennel Club recognized the breed in 1936. Another name people call the American Staffordshire Terrier is Am Staff or American Staffy.

Differences between the American Staffordshire Terrier and the Staffordshire Bull Terrier

Besides the difference that the Am Staff is from the United States and the Staffordshire Bull Terrier is from England there are other differences.
These two breeds are very similar but there is a big difference in their sizes:

  • The Am Staff generally measures 17 to 18 inches if it is a female and 18-19 if it is a male.
  • While the Staffordshire Bull Terrier being smaller, it usually measures from 14 to 16 inches.
  • Both breeds of dogs are considered medium sized.

How is the height of the dogs determined?

  • The height of the dogs is measured from the legs to the highest area of the shoulders.

Weight and LifeSpan

  • The Am Staff generally ranges in weight from 55-70 pounds for males and 40-55 pounds for females.
  • While the Staffordshire Bull Terrier has a weight of 28-38 pounds if male and 24-34 pounds if female.
  • The life span of the Am Staff is 12-16 years.
  • The life span of the Staffordshire Bull Terrier is 12-14 years.
